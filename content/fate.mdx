You ever notice how dangerous this sentence is?

"Don’t plan too much… it’s already written."

The moment we hear it, something inside us relaxes.
Not in a peaceful way.
In a surrendering way.
It feels comforting.
If it’s already written, then I don’t have to try that hard.
If it’s already written, then my failures aren’t really mine.
If it’s already written, then my effort doesn’t matter.

And that right there is how a passive life begins.
Psychology calls this an external locus of control — believing your life is controlled by fate, destiny, or some force outside you.
Stay there long enough, and it becomes something worse: learned helplessness.
You stop trying because you don’t believe trying changes anything.

But think about it.
If everything is fixed…
Then struggle has no meaning.
Growth has no credit.
Failure has no lesson.
Success has no ownership.
You’re not living.
You’re just acting out a script.

That’s why I respect action-based philosophies so much.
In the Bhagavad Gita, the idea wasn’t “sit back and let destiny decide.”
It was Karma Yoga — you are responsible for your actions, not the results.

Act.
Detach from the outcome, but never detach from effort.

Even within Hindu philosophy, destiny was never pure fatalism. There’s Prarabdha Karma — tendencies from the past. And there’s Purushartha — conscious effort in the present.
Translation?
You may have certain inclinations.
But you still choose how you respond.
That’s power.
And this is why the mindset matters.

If you walk through life believing everything is already written, you shrink.
You hesitate.
You avoid responsibility.
But if you believe your actions shape your reality — even within constraints — you grow.
That’s an internal locus of control.
That’s ownership.
That’s meaning.

Life only feels meaningless when you think you’re not participating in it.
The healthiest mindset isn’t “I control everything.”
And it isn’t “I control nothing.”
It’s this:
You don’t control everything.
But you are responsible for how you act within what you’re given.
Meaning requires participation.

So no…
It’s not written.
You’re writing it.
